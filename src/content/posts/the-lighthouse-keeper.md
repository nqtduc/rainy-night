Every night for forty years, Old Bao climbed the spiral stairs and lit the lamp.
He did not do it for the ships. The ships had stopped coming long ago, rerouted
to the new harbor in the south.

He did it because somewhere, he believed, there was still one boat that had not
come home. And on the night it finally rounded the point, he wanted the light to
be burning.

People in the village thought he was a little mad. Maybe he was. But every
evening the children watched the top of the tower, and every evening, right on
time, the light came on — steady, patient, certain.

When Bao died, the village kept the lamp lit. Just in case.
